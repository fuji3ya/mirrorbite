Nutrition Sources

Last updated: 2026-06-02

Directional, not medical advice. Mirrorbite gives a categorical, directional read of a meal (a 0–100 balanced-eating index plus four axes: protein, carbohydrate balance, fiber, and fat balance). It never reports gram or calorie numbers and is not a substitute for a registered dietitian or licensed health professional. The general nutrition principles our feedback is built on come from the public-health sources below.

The principles behind our feedback

Mirrorbite's "good / caution / low" axes and its one-line suggestions ("add a side of beans for fiber", "balance with more vegetables") reflect widely published, consensus public-health guidance on balanced eating — adequate lean protein, a balance of carbohydrate sources, sufficient dietary fiber and vegetables, and favouring healthy fats in moderation over heavily fried or saturated-fat-dominant meals. We do not invent numbers or make individualized medical claims.

Primary sources

  • Dietary Guidelines for Americans, 2020–2025 — U.S. Departments of Agriculture and Health & Human Services. dietaryguidelines.gov
  • Healthy Eating Plate — Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health, The Nutrition Source. hsph.harvard.edu/nutritionsource
  • Healthy diet — Fact sheet — World Health Organization (WHO). who.int/healthy-diet
  • Dietary fiber and health — Dietary Reference Intakes, National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine. nationalacademies.org

On the accuracy of photo-based estimates

Independent research has repeatedly shown that estimating nutrition from a single photo carries a wide margin of error (commonly cited at roughly ±30% or more). This is exactly why Mirrorbite stays directional — categorical bands and qualitative suggestions — instead of presenting false-precision calorie or gram figures, and why we hold back a result when a photo can't be read clearly.
